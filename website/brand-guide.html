Brand & photography guide

Everything your team needs to keep Help Mobility looking consistent, warm and trustworthy — colours, type, logo, voice, and exactly how to shoot photos for the website.

Colour

Palette

Warm and human, not clinical. Pine green leads; ochre is the accent for calls-to-action and highlights. Use cream as the background, never stark white.

Rule of thumb: pine for trust, ochre for action. Keep ochre to ~10% of any layout — buttons, key numbers, small highlights.
Typography

Type

A characterful serif for headlines, a clean humanist sans for everything else.

Display — Fraunces (600)
Mobility & independence at home
Body — Hanken Grotesk (400 / 600 / 700)

We handle the funding paperwork so you pay less and worry less. This is bold (600–700) for emphasis. Body text stays large and highly readable — our customers are seniors and people with disabilities.

Accessibility first: minimum body size 18px; never go below 16px. Maintain strong contrast (our palette already meets WCAG AA). Don’t set long text in the serif.
Logo

Logo usage

Help Mobility

Do

  • Keep clear space around the mark
  • Use on cream, white, or pine backgrounds
  • Use the wheel-of-independence mark on its own as an app/social icon

✕ Don’t

  • Recolour the logo outside the palette
  • Stretch, rotate or add shadows
  • Place on busy photos without a panel behind it
Voice & tone

How we sound

Warm, clear, and reassuring — like a knowledgeable friend, never a salesperson or a bureaucrat.

Do say

  • “We handle the paperwork.”
  • “Let’s find what you qualify for.”
  • “Independence at home.”
  • Plain words, short sentences.

✕ Avoid

  • Jargon and acronyms without explanation
  • Over-promising coverage (“free!”, “100% guaranteed”)
  • Cold, clinical or pushy language
  • Pity or fear-based messaging
Photography

Photo style

Real people, real homes, natural light. Photography is what makes the site feel human — these placeholders are marked throughout the site and should be replaced with your own.

Do shoot

  • Real clients (with written consent) in their own homes
  • Warm, natural daylight; bright and airy
  • Genuine moments — a smile, a caregiver helping, going outdoors
  • Equipment in real use, clean and well-lit
  • Diverse ages, backgrounds and abilities
  • Horizontal framing with room for text overlay

✕ Avoid

  • Generic stock photos that feel staged
  • Cold hospital or clinical settings
  • Harsh flash, dark or cluttered backgrounds
  • Faces without consent; isolating or sad framing
  • Heavy filters that distort skin tones

Shot list for the website

Capture these and you can replace every photo placeholder on the site:

1
Hero — homepage

A client at home with their equipment (wheelchair/scooter), smiling, warm daylight. Leave space on one side for the headline. Horizontal, high-res.

2
Four product categories

One clean photo each: mobility, a stairlift/ramp in a real home, a bathroom-safety setup, and daily-living aids in use.

3
Delivery & setup

A team member delivering/installing and showing a client how to use the equipment — friendly, professional, branded shirt.

4
Hospital-to-home

A calm homecoming scene — equipment set up and ready, caregiver present.

5
Team & storefront

Friendly portraits of staff and the showroom for the About section — builds local trust.

6
Headshots for testimonials

Optional client/partner headshots to pair with real reviews (with consent).

Always get written consent before photographing clients or sharing their stories. Keep image files optimized (≤300 KB each) so the site stays fast.

For developers

Quick technical notes

  • Shared styles live in assets/styles.css; shared behaviour in assets/site.js.
  • To connect forms, set FORM_ENDPOINT at the top of site.js (Formspree/Basin/etc.). Until then forms open the visitor’s email app.
  • Colours and fonts are CSS variables at the top of styles.css — change them in one place.
  • Replace [ Phone ], tel:+10000000000, the email and address across pages before launch.
